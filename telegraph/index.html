Curious to try it out himself, Edelcrantz built Sweden's first optical telegraph in 1794. So how did it work? Each flap on the telegraph represented a 3-bit binary number. A tenth flap showed the letter "A" before each code, making a total of 1,024 possible signals. With the help of a codebook, these signals could be translated into letters, numbers, syllables, or even full phrases.

Surprisingly, the system was powerful â€” with the right flap size, signals could reach up to 32 kilometers away. And it wasn't just for daytime use: Edelcrantz designed it to work at night too, by placing lamps behind the flaps. Nighttime codes were basically the opposite of the daytime ones, so messages could still get through after dark. (Text adapted from Wikipedia â€” see Optical telegraph.
